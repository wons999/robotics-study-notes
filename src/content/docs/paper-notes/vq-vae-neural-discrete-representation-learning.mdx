## One-line Summary

VQ-VAE learns discrete latent codes by combining autoencoding with vector quantization, giving neural models compact symbolic-like representations.

## Problem Setting

- Data: images, videos, speech, and other high-dimensional signals.
- Objective: learn useful unsupervised discrete representations.
- Model: encoder, vector quantizer/codebook, decoder, and learned prior.

## Core Idea

Instead of mapping inputs into continuous Gaussian latents, VQ-VAE maps encoder outputs to the nearest learned codebook vector. This creates discrete latent tokens that can be modeled by an autoregressive prior or used as compact representations.

## Method Details

- Architecture: encoder, vector quantization codebook, decoder, prior model.
- Training recipe: reconstruction objective plus codebook and commitment losses.
- Data: demonstrated across several modalities.
- Inference / control loop: not a control paper; used by T-Rex to compress tactile force histories.

## What I Should Remember

- T-Rex uses a VQ-VAE-like encoder to turn recent force histories into compact tactile tokens.
- Discrete tactile tokens can reduce noise and drift compared with raw high-frequency force streams.
- Codebook collapse and underused codes are practical issues to watch.

## Limitations

- Discretization may lose fine continuous detail.
- Codebook design and training stability matter.
- The original paper is not robotics-specific.
